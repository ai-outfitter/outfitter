Post-quantum resistant encryption refers to cryptographic schemes designed to remain secure against adversaries equipped with large-scale fault-tolerant quantum computers, which would break widely deployed public-key systems such as RSA, finite-field Diffie-Hellman, and elliptic-curve cryptography via Shor’s algorithm. Modern post-quantum encryption primarily relies on mathematical problems for which no efficient classical or quantum attacks are known, especially lattice-based constructions such as module learning with errors, as standardized in mechanisms like ML-KEM/Kyber for key encapsulation. In practice, post-quantum migration usually means replacing or hybridizing existing key exchange and public-key encryption components while retaining symmetric encryption and hashing with appropriately sized parameters, since Grover’s algorithm only gives a quadratic speedup against brute force. Key engineering concerns include larger keys and ciphertexts, side-channel resistance, implementation maturity, protocol compatibility, crypto-agility, and protecting data harvested today that may be decrypted later once quantum capabilities improve.
