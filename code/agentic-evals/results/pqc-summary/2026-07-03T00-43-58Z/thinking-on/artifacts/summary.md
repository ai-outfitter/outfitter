Post-quantum resistant encryption refers to cryptographic schemes designed to remain secure against adversaries with large-scale quantum computers, which could break widely deployed public-key systems such as RSA and elliptic-curve cryptography using Shor’s algorithm. The main practical focus is migrating key establishment and digital signatures to algorithms based on hard problems not known to be efficiently solvable by quantum algorithms, including lattice-based constructions such as ML-KEM/Kyber and ML-DSA/Dilithium, hash-based signatures, code-based cryptography, and multivariate or isogeny-derived approaches with varying maturity and trade-offs. Symmetric encryption is less directly threatened, though Grover’s algorithm effectively reduces brute-force security, so larger key sizes such as AES-256 are typically sufficient. In real systems, post-quantum adoption requires hybrid deployments, careful protocol integration, side-channel-resistant implementations, larger keys and ciphertexts, algorithm agility, and planning for “harvest now, decrypt later” attacks where encrypted traffic captured today may be decrypted once quantum capabilities mature.
